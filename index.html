Unexpected Call To Serve

In the year 1863, a young Belgian missionary stood at a crossroads of destiny. Saint Damien's brother, who was originally assigned to the Hawaiian mission, suddenly fell ill. With unwavering courage and deep faith, Damien stepped forward to replace his brother, forever altering the course of his life and the lives of countless others.

This pivotal moment would lead him to the remote shores of Hawaii, where his legacy of compassion and service would be forged in the most challenging circumstances imaginable.

Arrival to Molokai

The island of Molokai stood as a place of exile and despairâ€”a quarantine zone created by the Hawaiian government to isolate those afflicted with leprosy. When Father Damien first set foot on its shores in 1873, he became the first priest to venture willingly into what many called "The Colony of Death."

His arrival marked a turning point for the abandoned residents. Here was a man who chose to walk among them when others fled, who saw their humanity when society had cast them aside. His presence brought not only spiritual guidance but a renewed sense of dignity to those who had been forgotten.

Impact on the People: Carpenter Priest

Father Damien's hands were not only folded in prayer but also calloused from labor. A skilled carpenter by training, he put these practical talents to transformative use in Molokai. He built chapels, homes, and coffins, training the residents to work alongside him in these construction projects.

The rectory he constructed still stands today as a testament to his practical compassion. Through his carpentry, Father Damien gave the colony not just buildings but a sense of community and purpose. The sound of his hammer echoed his enduring message: that meaningful work and dignified living conditions were essential parts of a full life, even in the most difficult circumstances.

Fearless and Compassionate Toward the Lepers

In an era when leprosy inspired terror and sufferers were often abandoned, Father Damien's approach was revolutionary. He ate with the afflicted, dressed their wounds, and shared their living spaces without hesitation. "I make myself a leper with the lepers to gain all to Jesus Christ," he wrote to his brother.

His fearlessness in the face of a disease with no known cure at the time stood as a powerful testament to his faith and humanity. For sixteen years, he lived among the people of Molokai, creating a community where before there had only been isolation. Eventually, he contracted leprosy himself, becoming one with the very people he served until his death in 1889.
